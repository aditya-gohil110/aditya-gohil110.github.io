03 — PROCESS

How I think

01

Understand before I fix

I try to know why something's actually failing before I start changing things. It's saved me from patching the wrong problem more than once.

02

Plan for things going wrong

Systems break eventually. I try to build so that recovery doesn't depend on someone noticing at 2am.

03

Check assumptions with real feedback

I've learned not to fully trust my own guesses about what's useful. Talking to whoever's actually using something usually teaches me something I missed.

04

Make it make sense to others

Technical work doesn't help much if no one can act on it. I try to explain what I build in a way that's actually useful to the person reading it.

06 — ABOUT

Who I am

I studied Computer Science in India before moving to New York for a Master's in Information Systems. I thought I'd eventually have to pick between machine learning, backend systems, or mobile development. Turns out, I don't always have to.

Most of the projects I've enjoyed have had one thing in common: they started out a little messy. A model that behaved differently every week. A backend that worked right up until real users showed up. A workflow held together by good intentions and sticky notes. I like being the person who figures out why it's messy before trying to make it better.

I'm generally skeptical of quick fixes, surprisingly patient with debugging, and mildly suspicious of any system that claims to be "production-ready" without earning it first. That's probably why I enjoy building things that are meant to stick around, not just survive the demo.
